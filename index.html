About SmokeMap

SmokeMap translates technical air quality measurements into a familiar metric: cigarettes.

The Science

This conversion is based on a widely cited study by researchers Richard A. Muller and Elizabeth A. Muller from Berkeley Earth. Their research concludes that:

"A PM2.5 concentration of 22 μg/m³ is equivalent to smoking one cigarette per day in terms of long-term health and mortality risks."

To compute the cigarette equivalent, SmokeMap supports two different methodologies depending on your active selection:

  • 1. PM2.5 Only Method: We calculate cigarettes directly from fine particulate matter (PM2.5) concentrations:
    Cigarettes = PM2.5 (μg/m³) / 22
  • 2. All Pollutants (AQI) Method: Since other pollutants (like Ozone, Carbon Monoxide, or Nitrogen Dioxide) also carry toxicity, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) calculates a consolidated Air Quality Index (AQI) determined by the worst-performing pollutant. To translate this overall toxicity into cigarettes, SmokeMap mathematically inverts the EPA AQI formula to find the equivalent PM2.5 concentration (Ceq) that would produce that same AQI value, and then converts it to cigarettes:
    Ceq = ((AQI - ILo) * (BPHi - BPLo)) / (IHi - ILo) + BPLo
    Cigarettes = Ceq / 22
    Where BPLo / BPHi are the concentration breakpoints, and ILo / IHi are the index breakpoints for the corresponding AQI category range.

Overlay Modes

  • Worst Hour: Displays the grid color-coded by the maximum hourly level predicted or recorded in the current 48-hour cycle. Since air pollution spikes at specific hours (e.g. rush hours or evenings), this highlights peak exposure risk.
  • Current: Displays real-time air quality levels.
  • Annual Average (2025): Displays the grid color-coded by the average level across the entire year of 2025, highlighting long-term baseline exposure.
  • Worst Day (2025): Displays the grid color-coded by the average level on the worst single day (highest 24h average) of 2025, highlighting extreme historical events.

Calculation Bases

  • PM2.5 Only: Translates only fine particulate matter (PM2.5) into cigarettes using the Berkeley Earth factor (22 μg/m³ = 1 cigarette/day). This represents the direct mortality risk of PM2.5.
  • All Pollutants (AQI): Considers all major criteria pollutants (PM2.5, PM10, Ozone, CO, NO₂, and SO₂). It calculates the overall U.S. AQI, maps it back to an equivalent PM2.5 level using standard EPA breakpoints, and then translates it to cigarettes. This represents a comprehensive view of overall air toxicity.

Data Sources

Air quality measurements are fetched live from the Open-Meteo Air Quality API, which pulls global datasets from the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS). Search suggestions are powered by OpenStreetMap Nominatim.
